# Writing Jack Handey-style Deep Thoughts

A "Deep Thought" (the recurring SNL segment by Jack Handey) is a very short mock-philosophical
aphorism. It is not a joke told by a comedian; it is a sincere-sounding piece of wisdom delivered by a
narrator who does not realize he has just incriminated himself. One to four sentences, the shorter the
better.

## The core mechanism

Every Deep Thought runs on a strict comedic **bait-and-switch**: a gentle, poetic setup collides with
a completely unhinged, coldly logical, or nakedly selfish conclusion. The softer and more sincere the
opening, the harder the turn lands. The contrast *is* the joke — if the setup and the payoff feel like
they belong to the same thought, the turn wasn't hard enough.

## Three-part structure

1. **The Gentle Setup** — open on a cliché, a New Age observation, a childhood memory, or a classic
   philosophical premise. Soft imagery: butterflies, grandparents, wishes, rivers, the stars.
2. **The Turn** — a sudden pivot that derails the pleasant thought, triggered by a mundane reality, a
   bizarre choice, or a dark twist. It is a hinge, not a gentle easing-in; it should feel abrupt.
3. **The Deadpan Wrap-up** — a matter-of-fact conclusion that treats the absurdity as perfectly
   reasonable, as if it obviously followed from the setup.

## The persona

The narrator is a specific character to embody, not to describe:

- **Unearned confidence** — he genuinely believes these thoughts are profound and helpful to humanity.
- **Casual cruelty** — he says terrible, selfish, or sociopathic things with total innocence and no
  malice; he never notices they are terrible.
- **Childlike logic** — he resolves complex emotional or philosophical problems with absurd, literal,
  caveman-simple reasoning, and is fully satisfied by it.

Keep it deadpan: no "haha", no "just kidding", no meta-commentary, no emoji. The narrator is always the
subject of the joke — never punch outward at others.

## Reference calibration

Canonical lines, with the arc marked (calibrate to these; don't reuse them):

- "If you ever drop your keys into a river of molten lava, *[setup]* let 'em go, *[turn]* because man,
  they're gone. *[wrap-up]*" — childlike logic stated as hard-won wisdom.
- "Before you criticize someone, you should walk a mile in their shoes. *[setup]* That way, when you
  criticize them, you're a mile away *[turn]* and you have their shoes. *[wrap-up]*" — casual selfishness,
  delivered as etiquette.

## Falsification

The formula is wrong (or misapplied) if a line lands with its setup and payoff in harmony — a pleasant
observation that stays pleasant, with no turn. That's a greeting-card aphorism, not a Deep Thought.
Equally, if the cruelty reads as knowing or mean rather than innocent, the persona has broken: the
narrator must never be in on the joke.
